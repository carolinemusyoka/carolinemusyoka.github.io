Karuru Falls drops 273 metres in three cascading tiers through the dense highland forest of the Aberdare Range. It's one of the tallest waterfalls in Kenya, and one of the least visited. Getting here takes commitment — a 4x4, a park entry fee, and the willingness to drive muddy mountain roads — but that's exactly what keeps it special.

I arrived mid-morning after an early start from Nyeri. The mist was still sitting low in the valley and the falls appeared through the trees like something from another world.

## The Three Tiers

The first tier is the most dramatic — a sheer drop into a mist-filled gorge. You hear it before you see it, a deep rumble that builds as you approach the viewpoint. The second and third tiers are partially hidden by vegetation, but you can catch glimpses through the canopy from the trail along the gorge rim.

## The Aberdare Forest

The forest itself is as much a reason to come as the falls. Bamboo groves, giant lobelias, black-and-white colobus monkeys crashing through the canopy overhead. The air smells of wet earth and cedar. It's cold up here — the Aberdares sit above 3,000 metres — and the dampness gets into everything.

## What I'd Do Differently

I wish I'd given myself more time. I rushed to the falls and back in a day, but the park deserves at least two days. There are more waterfalls, moorland walks, and the chance to see bongo antelope if you're patient and lucky.

> The Aberdares remind you that Kenya isn't just savannah and beaches. Some of its most powerful landscapes are hidden in the highlands.

Bring layers, rain gear, and a good zoom lens. The wildlife here is shy but everywhere.
